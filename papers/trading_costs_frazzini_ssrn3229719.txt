Trading Costs
Frazzini, Israel, and Moskowitz
SSRN: 3229719

ABSTRACT
Using 1.7 trillion dollars of live trade execution data from a large institutional money manager across 21 developed equity markets over a 19-year period, we measure the real-world trading costs and price impact function of a large trader. We provide a novel description of how costs vary across trade type, stock characteristics, trade size, time, and exchanges globally to test various theories of price impact. We find actual trading costs to be an order of magnitude smaller than previous studies suggest, and describe the trading process leading to these costs. A model calibrated to match the distribution of actual costs across trade size, stocks, and time outperforms other models from the literature in out of sample tests that attempt to describe independent costs from brokers and realized costs of traded index funds. Our model based on realized costs from live trades portrays very different implementation costs than previous studies suggest.

KEY FINDINGS

1. MEASURING TRADING COSTS
- Price impact (MI) is measured as the difference between execution price and arrival price (pre-trade benchmark), scaled by arrival price.
- Decomposed into temporary (MI_temp) and permanent (MI_perm) components.
  - MI_temp = reversal of price impact on the following trading day
  - MI_perm = MI - MI_temp
- Also computed relative to VWAP: MI_relative = Q_ex(P_ex - VWAP)

2. WHAT DRIVES TRADING COSTS

A. Market Structure (across 21 equity markets, 32 exchanges)
- Intraday triggered auctions INCREASE costs by ~1.89 bps (median) to ~3 bps (mean)
- Number of LIT venues DECREASES costs by ~1.48 bps per dollar traded
- OTC trading availability reduces average costs by more than 4 bps
- Transaction taxes, short-sale uptick rules: limited significant impact due to algorithmic adjustment
- For largest trades (value-weighted), most market structure features lose significance

B. Price Impact Model (Table VII specification)
Key variables:
- Beta * IndexRet * buysell: controls for contemporaneous market movement
- Log(Market Equity): larger stocks have lower costs
- Fraction of daily volume (linear + square root): sqrt term consistently ~8-12 bps
- Idiosyncratic Volatility: higher vol → higher costs (~0.24-0.32 bps per % vol)
- VIX: higher market volatility → higher costs
- DGTW-adjusted return * buysell: ~0.27 coefficient, highly significant

C. Sub-period robustness (Table A6)
- Sqrt(fraction of daily volume) is the most robust predictor across all periods:
  1998-2005: ~9.23 | 2006-2016: ~8.23 | 2007-2009: ~11.71 | 2010-2016: ~7.39
- DGTW return coefficient (~0.27) is remarkably stable across all periods and regions
- Log(ME) becomes reliably negative (larger = cheaper) only from 2006 onward

D. Comparison to literature measures (Table VIII)
- TAQ Lambda (Kyle's lambda) adds marginal R² of 0.0008 (US)
- Modified Roll measure: significant univariate but not in multivariate
- Amihud, PropZero, TAQ Effective Spread: not significant in multivariate
- Conclusion: live execution data reveals much lower costs than indirect proxies suggest

3. INTRADAY PATTERNS
- Average price impact for US trades: event-time evolution shows temporary component reverting within ~1 trading day
- Permanent component reflects genuine information transmission

4. KEY TAKEAWAY
Actual institutional trading costs are far smaller than academic proxies imply. The dominant cost drivers are trade size (as fraction of daily volume, via square-root law) and stock-level idiosyncratic volatility. Market structure matters at the margin but sophisticated algorithms largely neutralize most frictions.
